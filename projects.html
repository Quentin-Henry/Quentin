If you saw a lanternfly would you kill it?

The lanternfly project began as a response to a feeling of hesitation. The first summer the swarm of invasive insects decided on New York, I found myself in a position to kill one. I had heard that you should kill them since they damage the local ecosystem. But with my foot over the small creature, I found myself stuck. I didn't want to kill it. I was sent into a spiral of questioning, would I kill it if it was ugly or if it was a biting insect? Am I really stepping on this bug because I was told to do so and I never once thought to do any research? I soon realized I was beginning to be confronted with something that doesn't have a simple name.

The overwhelming weight of everything that has ever happened.

This insect sat at the metaphorical tip of the iceberg, and under the surface was a chain of events that started long before you or I was born and will likely continue after. Histories of global trade, westward expansion, colonialism, chinoiserie gardens, climate change, and the Cold War all accumulated into a single moment where a bug from southeast Asia sat under the shadow of my foot in New York City. But anything can be like this if you think about it. Even the smallest decisions can come to contain the weight of everything that has happened and everything that has yet to happen. And in this moment it is easy to feel insignificant and powerless. Like an insect. Maybe it's something that is best to not think about. Step on the bug, or don't. What difference will it make?

The Lanternfly Character Design

In the winter when lanternflies no longer fill the air, the mascot was created as both a reminder of their lasting presence and also as a way to think about this web of connections. What would It mean for the lantern fly to become a symbol for New York? Although a relatively new co-inhabitant of the city, the insect has already made a lasting impression, it is invasive, stylish, clumsy, and hitchhiked all the way to New York. A Series of Paradoxes

Calatrava: Against Gravity is an identity design project for a fictional exhibition featuring the architectural drawings of the architect Santiago Calatrava. While Calatrava's architecture is fundamentally inspired by the human body, it is hard to imagine it being grounded by any means. His structures often appear as spaceships ready for flight. With Intricate detailing and massive asymmetrical forms, the structures often appear gravity-defying, pushing the limits of engineering and technology. Sometimes even at the price of structural stability. Calatrava's work is then built by a series of paradoxes. Structures that are both simple and complex, light and heavy, balanced and imbalanced, ancient and futuristic, modest and arrogant.

Many of Calatrava's works are surrounded by reflective pools. Others accentuate their asymmetry.

Buildings and Cities

The logo design seeks to reflect Calatrava's work and the paradoxes it contains. Built from the body of Helvetica regular and italic, it creates complexity from simplicity and combines balance and imbalance. The blue is symbolic of a blueprint or an architectural plan as well as the blue sky that always surrounds and juxtaposes the stark white of Calatrava’s structures both above and in the reflection pools below.

The logo variations can be combined to create ‘buildings’. The buildings can be combined to create cities. This allows the possibility of infinite non-repeating patterns. The modularity allows for quick and easy resizing for any composition.

Shape Generator

Derived from the logo, the shape generator can be used as a framework for the identity system, arrows, numbers, and icons, can all be created with the same line weight and angles creating a cohesive system with a simple and straightforward way of making additions

Christina curtis lmft is a couples therapist based out of New York City, The waves, coded with paper.js symbolize the interaction of three people, emotional wavelengths and communication, as well as a sense of relaxation, like waves onto a beach or the floating amorphous forms of a lava lamp. I started making work about New York when I realized I've only ever made work about New York

I was going to school at the Cooper Union a block from where I was born and began to feel a growing claustrophobia. Through space and time, I was traveling in circles, caught in an orbit around an indescribable mass located at the intersection of 7th Street and Second Avenue. I made a decision to leave and headed east for 6 months, a straight line that led back to where it began. The whole time I got the feeling that I never really left New York. I found it down city streets, in flood plains, and river valleys, in the air between mountains that rises up the slopes. It can be found when someone's speaking to you in a language you can't begin to understand, it's on the border guards wrist watch, and in the rock you take from the beach that gets lost in your bag. When I got back I started making work about this experience, trying to think about how we navigate through our memories and the places they exist. This project is still in progress.
